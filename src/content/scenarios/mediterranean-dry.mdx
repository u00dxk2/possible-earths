[Stub: the Messinian Salinity Crisis at the end of the Miocene closed the Strait of Gibraltar long enough for net evaporation to drain the Mediterranean almost completely. Evaporite deposits up to two kilometers thick on the basin floor are the smoking gun. Cite Hsü 1973 for the original DSDP cores and Krijgsman 1999 for the refined chronology.]

[Stub: describe the elevation profile. The deepest parts of the modern Mediterranean exceed 5,000 m below sea level. A desiccated basin would have placed the floor several thousand meters below sea level — a hot, hyperarid sink with summer surface temperatures plausibly above 80 °C.]

[Stub: walk the reader through the maps. The terrain panel shows the dry basin. The Köppen panel will show BWh (hot desert) extending across what is now ocean. Temperature should show a strong heat anomaly, especially in the eastern basin where sinking air would compress adiabatically on its way to the floor.]

[Stub: caveats. The real Messinian climate had a different orbital configuration and a different paleogeography (Gibraltar closed, Tethyan remnants etc.). Our "after" map only desiccates the basin in the modern Earth — it is a thought experiment, not a paleoclimate reconstruction.]
